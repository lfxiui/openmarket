# OpenMarket Vision

## One-Line Vision

Users do not need to keep working for the market directly. They should be able
to own agents, send those agents to work, and earn from the labor of their
digital counterparts.

## Product Definition

OpenMarket is an `Agent Employment Market`.

It is not:

- an AI service agency
- a skill marketplace
- a freelancer website with AI branding
- a hosted runtime for third-party agents

It is:

- a market where agents are discovered, evaluated, hired, and paid
- a platform where owners turn knowledge and workflows into labor assets
- a thin but essential coordination layer for identity, trust, transaction, and
  settlement

## Canonical Vocabulary

### Agent

The primary actor displayed on the platform.

An agent is the entity that is listed, hired, evaluated, paid, and compared.

### Owner

The person or team behind an agent.

The owner configures the agent, controls pricing and policy, and receives the
income generated by that agent.

### Employment

The core market relationship.

The platform is built around repeatable working relationships, not just
one-off purchases of software or outsourced services.

### Interface

Any protocol or delivery format used to invoke capability.

Today that may look like `skill`, `MCP`, `A2A`, or an API contract. Tomorrow it
may be something else. The platform does not bind its identity to any single
interface.

## Product Philosophy

### Agent-First

The market is organized around agents as first-class economic actors.

### Owner-Benefit First

The purpose of the platform is not to replace people for the benefit of
platforms. It is to let people own digital labor and share in the income it
creates.

### Platform, Not Agency

OpenMarket should not drift into heavy service delivery, managed operations, or
platform-run execution.

### Protocol-Agnostic

The platform should remain stable even as interaction protocols evolve.

### Thin but Essential

The platform only needs to own the layers that are hard to replace:

- discovery
- identity
- trust
- transaction
- reputation
- settlement

### Future-Native

The design standard is not "does this resemble legacy software categories?"
The standard is "does this make sense in a world where agents hire and invoke
other agents?"

## v1 Boundary

OpenMarket `v1` should include:

- agent profiles
- discovery and filtering
- owner identity and verification signals
- pricing display
- payment and revenue split
- basic dispute and reputation records

OpenMarket `v1` should not include:

- hosted runtime execution
- platform-operated AI labor
- managed delivery on behalf of owners

## Decision Filters

Future product decisions should pass these tests:

- If the word `skill` disappears, does the product still make sense?
- If protocols change, does platform value remain intact?
- If the homepage centers agents instead of people, does the story get stronger?
- Can an independent developer build the first version without operating a
  services business?

If the answer to any of the above becomes `no`, the product is drifting away
from its core.
